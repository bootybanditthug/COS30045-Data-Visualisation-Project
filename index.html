Scale

How big is the enforcement system, and has it grown over 17 years?

Australian agencies conduct millions of roadside tests every year — but the volume is not evenly distributed across states, and it has not been constant over time. Some years saw large surges; 2020 saw a dramatic collapse. Filtering by state or year shows how enforcement intensity has shifted.

Coverage

Which states test the most — and does volume predict safer outcomes?

The eight jurisdictions vary enormously in testing scale. Some conduct tens of millions of tests per year; others far fewer. But raw volume does not automatically translate to lower positivity rates — the relationship between effort and outcome is the core puzzle this site investigates.

Story 1 · National trend

More testing, fewer drunk drivers — enforcement is working

Australia's national drink-driving positivity rate fell 52% between 2008 and 2019 — from 0.89% to 0.43% — even as test volumes grew by only 19%. Behaviour shifted, not just detection.

Story 2 · COVID-19 disruption

COVID reversed a decade of progress in a single year

When lockdowns halved breath testing volumes — from 15.2 million tests in 2019 to just 7.3 million in 2020 — the national positivity rate spiked from 0.43% back to 0.75%. That is the largest single-year increase in the 16-year dataset, reversing eight years of sustained improvement almost overnight.

Story 3 · NT outlier

The Northern Territory is a persistent crisis in its own category

NT's drink-driving positivity rate was 7.17% in 2023 — more than 13 times the national average of 0.53%. Despite 15 years of data, NT has barely improved from 8.47% in 2008. Without NT, Australia's national average would fall below 0.40%. The NT alone is masking how much every other state has improved.

Story 4 · WA success

Western Australia cut its rate by 81% — the nation's biggest turnaround

WA moved from 2.20% positivity in 2008 — the second-highest of any mainland state — to just 0.41% in 2023. That is the largest sustained improvement of any jurisdiction, and it demonstrates that dramatic change is achievable within the existing enforcement framework. South Australia's 54% drop is the second-strongest confirmation of the same pattern.

05 The Verdict

Does cracking down on road safety actually work?

Based on 16 years of national enforcement data, here is what the evidence shows.

Yes — sustained enforcement does change driver behaviour. But the effect is uneven, depends on consistency, and does not reach everywhere equally.

Story 1 · National trend

−52%

Positivity rate 2008 → 2019

National drink-driving positivity fell from 0.89% to 0.43% over 11 years while test volumes grew by only 19%. Behaviour shifted, not just detection.

Story 2 · COVID disruption

+74%

Positivity spike in 2020

When lockdowns halved testing volumes, positivity jumped from 0.43% to 0.75% in a single year — reversing eight years of progress in twelve months.

Story 3 · NT outlier

7.17%

NT positivity rate in 2023

More than 13× the national average of 0.53%. NT has barely moved from 8.47% in 2008 — a persistent crisis that national averages alone cannot capture.

Story 4 · WA success — the proof it can work

−81%

Western Australia's positivity rate, 2008 to 2023

WA moved from 2.20% positivity in 2008 — the second-highest of any mainland state — to just 0.41% in 2023. That is the largest sustained improvement of any jurisdiction and demonstrates that dramatic change is achievable within the existing enforcement framework. South Australia's 54% drop is the second-strongest confirmation.

Story 5 · Enforcement shift

+273%

Mobile phone fines, 2019 → 2022

From 97,000 to 362,000 fines in three years — driven by automated phone-detection cameras. A structural change in how road safety is policed, whose long-term behavioural effect is still emerging.

The data shows that enforcement does change behaviour — but the effect is not uniform and it is not automatic. Sustained testing over many years correlates with falling positivity rates in most states, and WA's 81% drop is the clearest evidence that dramatic improvement is achievable. But when enforcement collapses — as it did in 2020 — behaviour deteriorates rapidly, confirming that the deterrent effect requires ongoing visibility. The Northern Territory remains a persistent outlier that no national average can honestly represent. And the explosive growth of mobile phone fines after 2019 signals a structural shift in how road safety is policed — one whose long-term effect on behaviour is only beginning to show in the data.
